# Federal Water Heater Efficiency Standards — 2026 and 2029 Transitions

A reference for plumbing AI skills that touch on water heater sales, replacement, install, or customer education. These are the two federal rule changes that reshape the residential and commercial water heater market through the end of the decade, plus the federal tax credit status as of April 2026.

---

## Residential — DOE 10 CFR 430 final rule

**Effective date:** May 6, 2029, on equipment manufactured on or after that date.

**What changes:**
- Electric storage water heaters with rated storage volume **greater than 35 gallons** must use heat pump technology to meet new minimum Uniform Energy Factor (UEF) requirements. In practice, manufacturers will discontinue standard electric resistance tanks above 35 gallons in this timeframe.
- Electric storage water heaters between **20 and 35 gallons** see no UEF change but are limited to a maximum first-hour rating of 50 gallons and a maximum factory temperature setting of 135°F.
- Nominal 40-gallon electrics currently rated at 36 or 37 gallons will be reduced to 35 gallons to remain in the smaller class.
- Gas storage water heaters and tankless units have substantially different (and less restrictive) requirements; the headline residential rule does not force a fuel switch.

**What it does NOT change:**
- Homeowners are not required to replace working equipment.
- Existing inventory of compliant units installed before the effective date can continue in service indefinitely.
- The rule does not retroactively apply to installs completed before May 6, 2029.

**Practical implications for the plumbing shop:**
- Shops should be HPWH-install-capable by 2027–2028 to avoid losing the >35gal electric replacement market.
- HPWH installs require: dedicated 30A 240V circuit (in most cases), 700–1,000+ cubic feet of ambient air around the unit (or ducted air), condensate routing, and a footprint that accommodates a taller unit than the equivalent resistance tank.
- Cold-climate basement installs may pull the basement temperature down 3–5°F during heavy use cycles; this is usually fine but worth disclosing to customers.

## Commercial — DOE commercial water heater rule

**Effective date:** October 6, 2026, on equipment manufactured or imported on or after that date.

**What changes:**
- Commercial gas water heaters in the covered size class must meet **condensing efficiency** standards. Non-condensing commercial gas water heaters in the covered class can no longer be manufactured or imported on or after October 6, 2026.
- Existing non-condensing units in service can continue to operate; the rule does not force replacement.

**Enforcement delayed one year — DOE Enforcement Policy Statement, April 24, 2026 (added 2026-06-22):** On April 24, 2026, DOE issued an Enforcement Policy Statement exercising its enforcement discretion to **delay enforcement** of the thermal-efficiency standards for gas-fired storage water heaters, storage-type instantaneous water heaters, gas-fired instantaneous water heaters, and hot water supply boilers manufactured **on or after October 6, 2026 but before October 6, 2027**. The effect is a roughly one-year enforcement grace period (Oct 6, 2026 → Oct 6, 2027), driven by pending Supreme Court litigation over DOE's standards authority. DOE posted clarifications dated May 4, 2026 (energy.gov, Office of the General Counsel).

Three nuances matter for customer-facing accuracy:
- **The standard is not repealed.** The October 6, 2023 final rule still takes legal effect on October 6, 2026. What changed is enforcement posture — DOE has said it will not pursue enforcement against covered commercial gas products manufactured in the grace window. Manufacturers (e.g., A.O. Smith) have signaled they will **continue manufacturing and selling non-condensing commercial gas water heaters** during the window, so non-condensing commercial gas units are expected to remain available to purchase and install through roughly late 2027 rather than disappearing in October 2026.
- **Scope of the delay is commercial gas only.** Commercial electric water heaters (tank or tankless) and commercial oil-fired water heaters were never covered by this thermal-efficiency change and are unaffected by both the rule and the delay.
- **Litigation risk cuts both ways.** Because the delay rests on enforcement discretion tied to active litigation, it can shift. Treat the grace window as the current operating reality but not a guaranteed end state; re-check before making a multi-year capital-timing promise to a customer.

**What it does NOT change:**
- Residential electric heat-pump water heaters and the May 6, 2029 residential standard are unaffected by the commercial rule or its enforcement delay.
- Equipment outside the covered input range continues under separate rules.

**Practical implications for the plumbing shop:**
- A like-for-like replacement of a covered commercial gas unit installed in late 2026 or 2027 may now still be a **non-condensing model** while the enforcement grace period holds — but the shop should still walk the customer through the condensing transition, because (a) the grace period is litigation-contingent and could end early, and (b) a condensing unit installed now is future-proof against the standard whenever enforcement begins. When a customer does move to a **condensing model**, expect different vent material (PVC instead of B-vent or single-wall), different vent path, condensate management (typically a condensate trap and a neutralizer if discharging into copper or cast iron drains), and possibly different gas line sizing for the higher input rating.
- Capital-purchase planning conversations with property managers and facilities directors should still happen in Q2 and Q3 of 2026 — but the framing has shifted from "buy before the October 6, 2026 wall" to "the wall moved to roughly October 2027 on enforcement, so you have more runway than the headlines suggest; decide condensing-vs-non-condensing on total cost of ownership and litigation risk, not on a panic deadline."
- Vent path retrofits (B-vent through the roof to PVC sidewall) can be a significant install cost adder. Site-specific assessment matters.

## Federal tax credit status — Section 25C

**Status as of April 2026:** The Section 25C Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit **expired on December 31, 2025**. Heat pump and heat pump water heater installations completed in 2026 or later are not eligible for the federal tax credit.

**What is still available federally:**
- **HEEHRA** (High-Efficiency Electric Home Rebate Act) provides point-of-sale rebates for electric appliances including heat pumps and heat pump water heaters. Rollout is state-by-state and program activation varies — some states have active programs; others are still setting up administration.
- The **DOE Tax Credit Product Lookup Tool** at regulations.doe.gov is the source for product eligibility.
- **DSIRE** (Database of State Incentives for Renewables & Efficiency) is the canonical state-by-state and utility-by-utility incentive directory.

**Customer-conversation discipline:**
- Do not promise specific federal tax credit dollar amounts for 2026 installs. The 25C federal credit is not available.
- Do name state programs by name (TECH Clean California, Mass Save, NYSERDA, Energize CT, Focus on Energy, etc.) and utility programs by utility name. Final dollar amounts should be presented as "estimated — confirmed at install" because incentive programs change quarterly.
- HEEHRA point-of-sale rebates, where active, are processed by the contractor at install and netted off the customer's invoice.
